10 Compliance Mistakes Companies Make Under Japan’s Freelance Act

Laexis Advisory | Regulatory Compliance | Japan | 2026

Japan’s Freelance Act establishes a new compliance framework governing transactions between companies and freelancers. Businesses engaging independent professionals must comply with statutory obligations relating to contract documentation, payment deadlines, unfair trade practices, harassment prevention, and termination procedures.

1. Assuming the Law Only Applies to “Gig Workers”

A common misconception is that the Freelance Act primarily targets gig economy platforms. In practice, the law applies to a much broader category of outsourcing arrangements.

Independent consultants, designers, engineers, translators, writers, and other professional service providers may qualify as freelancers if they operate without regular employees or partners.

2. Relying on the Vendor’s Business Name

Another frequent mistake is assuming that a vendor’s business name or branding determines its legal classification.

A service provider calling itself a consulting firm or agency may still qualify as a freelancer if it is effectively operated by a single individual without employees.

3. Failing to Verify Whether Vendors Have Employees

The definition of a freelancer depends heavily on whether the service provider employs regular workers. However, many procurement teams do not collect this information during vendor onboarding.

Without verifying the vendor’s employment structure, companies cannot reliably determine whether the Act applies.

4. Using Incomplete or Informal Contracts

The Freelance Act requires companies to provide written or electronic agreements specifying key contractual elements such as scope of work, delivery timeline, inspection procedures, and payment terms.

  • Email exchanges
  • Purchase orders without detailed terms
  • Verbal agreements

These informal arrangements may fail to satisfy statutory requirements and increase the risk of disputes.

5. Ignoring Payment Deadline Rules

The Act establishes clear payment deadlines. As a general rule, companies must pay freelancers within 60 days after delivery or inspection of the work.

Companies that continue to apply internal payment cycles designed for other suppliers may inadvertently violate the Act.

6. Making Unilateral Changes to the Scope of Work

Project requirements often evolve. However, companies sometimes request additional work without adjusting compensation or revising contractual terms.

Any significant changes to project scope should therefore be clearly documented and mutually agreed upon.

7. Reducing Fees After the Work Has Begun

Another prohibited practice is the unjustified reduction of agreed fees after the project has already begun.

Businesses should treat agreed compensation as binding unless both parties voluntarily agree to modify the terms.

8. Overlooking Harassment Prevention Obligations

Freelancers may also be victims of workplace harassment connected to a company’s business environment.

  • Consultation channels
  • Complaint investigation procedures
  • Corrective measures

9. Abruptly Terminating Long-Term Freelance Contracts

For freelance engagements lasting more than six months, companies generally must provide at least 30 days’ advance notice before termination.

10. Treating Freelance Compliance as a Purely Legal Issue

Compliance with the Freelance Act involves multiple corporate functions including procurement, finance, human resources, and operational departments.

Effective compliance therefore requires cross-department coordination.

Conclusion

Japan’s Freelance Act introduces new compliance obligations for companies engaging external vendors. Businesses should review their vendor management processes and contractual practices to reduce legal and regulatory risks.

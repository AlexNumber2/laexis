10 Compliance Mistakes Companies Make Under Japan's Freelance Act

Laexis Advisory Regulatory Compliance Risk Japan 2026

Japan's Freelance Act (Act on the Properization of Transactions Involving Specified Commissioned Business Operators), effective November 2024, establishes a new compliance framework governing transactions between companies and freelancers. Businesses engaging independent professionals must now comply with statutory obligations relating to contract documentation, payment deadlines, unfair trade practices, harassment prevention, and termination procedures. This article highlights ten common compliance mistakes and outlines practical steps for building an effective compliance framework.

1. Assuming the Law Only Applies to "Gig Workers"

A common misconception is that the Freelance Act primarily targets gig economy platforms. In practice, the law applies to a much broader category of outsourcing arrangements. Independent consultants, designers, engineers, translators, writers, and other professional service providers may qualify as freelancers if they operate without regular employee(s) or partner(s).

Even licensed professionals such as attorneys or certified public accountants may qualify as freelancers under the Act. If such professionals do not employ regular staff or maintain work partners within their practice, they may fall within the statutory definition of a freelancer. This approach differs from many other jurisdictions, where professionals such as attorneys or CPAs are generally not regarded as vulnerable market participants requiring regulatory protection, as they typically possess relatively strong bargaining power compared with other categories of freelancers.

Another point requiring careful consideration concerns the employment of family members. Where a business operator formally hires a family member as a regular employee or work partner, the operator may nevertheless still be regarded as a freelancer under the Act. In practice, regulators may consider that the business operator and the family member share aligned economic interests, and therefore such arrangements do not necessarily remove the operator from the scope of the freelance classification.

2. Relying on the Vendor's Business Name

Another frequent mistake is assuming that a vendor's business name or branding determines its legal classification.

A service provider calling itself a "consulting firm," "agency," or "studio" may still qualify as a freelancer if the organization is effectively operated by a single individual without employee(s) or partner(s). The Freelance Act focuses on the substance of the business structure rather than its external label.

3. Failing to Verify Whether Vendors Have Employees

The definition of a freelancer depends heavily on whether the service provider employs regular workers. However, many procurement teams do not collect this information during vendor onboarding.

Without verifying the vendor's employment structure, companies cannot reliably determine whether the Act applies. A practical approach is to introduce vendor self-declaration procedures requesting information about employees, organizational structure, and business operations. If the vendor refuses to provide such information, it would be better to regard such vendor as a possible freelancer.

4. Using Incomplete or Informal Contracts

The Freelance Act requires companies to provide written or electronic agreements specifying key contractual elements such as the scope of work, delivery timeline, inspection procedures, and payment terms.

Despite this requirement, many companies still rely on informal arrangements such as:

  • Email exchanges
  • Purchase orders without detailed terms
  • Verbal agreements

Such arrangements may fail to satisfy statutory requirements and increase the risk of disputes.

5. Ignoring Payment Deadline Rules

The Act establishes clear payment deadlines. As a general rule, companies must pay freelancers within 60 days after delivery or inspection of the work. In certain subcontract-like situations, the deadline may be shorter.

Companies that continue to apply internal payment cycles designed for other suppliers, such as 90 days or longer, may inadvertently violate the Act.

6. Making Unilateral Changes to the Scope of Work

In many outsourcing relationships, project requirements evolve. However, companies sometimes request additional work without adjusting compensation or revising contractual terms.

The Freelance Act prohibits unfair practices, including unreasonable demands for additional work or unilateral changes to agreed terms. Any significant changes to project scope should therefore be clearly documented and mutually agreed upon.

7. Reducing Fees After the Work Has Begun

Another prohibited practice under the Act is the unjustified reduction of agreed fees. Some companies attempt to renegotiate compensation after a project has begun or after deliverables have been submitted.

Even when such adjustments appear commercially reasonable, unilateral fee reductions may be considered unfair treatment under the Act. Businesses should treat agreed compensation as binding unless both parties voluntarily agree to modify the terms.

8. Overlooking Harassment Prevention Obligations

The Freelance Act explicitly recognizes freelancers as potential victims of harassment connected to a company's business activities. Organizations engaging freelancers must therefore implement appropriate measures, including:

  • Consultation channels for reporting concerns
  • Procedures for investigating complaints
  • Corrective actions where necessary

Failing to extend harassment prevention policies to freelancers may create compliance risks.

9. Abruptly Terminating Long-Term Freelance Contracts

For freelance engagements lasting more than six months, the Act provides additional procedural protections. Companies must generally provide at least 30 days' advance notice before terminating or declining renewal of such contracts.

If the freelancer requests an explanation, the company must also provide the reasons for termination. Abrupt termination without proper notice may trigger regulatory scrutiny.

10. Treating Freelance Compliance as a Purely Legal Issue

Compliance with the Freelance Act cannot be handled solely by the legal department. Freelance engagements involve multiple corporate functions, including procurement, finance, human resources, and operational business units.

Effective compliance therefore requires cross-department coordination and clear internal procedures.

Building an Effective Freelance Compliance Framework

To reduce legal and operational risks, companies should consider implementing the following measures:

  • Vendor classification procedures to identify freelancers
  • Standardized contract templates reflecting statutory requirements
  • Vendor self-declaration systems confirming employment structure
  • Centralized vendor monitoring for oversight and compliance reviews
  • Cross-department coordination across legal, procurement, and finance

Conclusion

Japan's Freelance Act represents an important development in the regulation of independent service relationships. Although the law aims to promote fairness in freelance transactions, it also introduces new compliance obligations for businesses engaging external vendors.

Companies that continue to rely on outdated outsourcing practices may face increased regulatory and reputational risks. Organizations that proactively review their vendor management processes and contractual practices will be better positioned to navigate the evolving regulatory environment and maintain sustainable relationships with freelance professionals.
